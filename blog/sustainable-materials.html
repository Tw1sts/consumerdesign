Sustainable Materials in Product Design

Published on January 22, 2025 | By Michael Twist | Sustainable Design

The materials we choose define not just the aesthetic and functional qualities of our products, but increasingly their environmental impact. As consumer awareness grows and regulations tighten, sustainable materials have evolved from a nice-to-have feature to a business imperative. This article explores the innovative materials reshaping product design and how to incorporate them effectively.

Beyond Recycled Plastics: The New Generation of Sustainable Materials

While recycled plastics remain important, today's sustainable material landscape offers far more diverse and sophisticated options. Here are some of the most promising materials transforming product design:

Bioplastics and Biomaterials

Derived from renewable biomass sources like vegetable fats, cornstarch, or algae, next-generation bioplastics offer properties that rival conventional plastics. For a recent consumer electronics accessory project, we used PHA (polyhydroxyalkanoate) bioplastic, which offers the durability of ABS but biodegrades completely in marine environments.

Beyond plastics, biomaterials like mycelium (mushroom) composites are creating entirely new material categories. These fungi-based materials can be grown into custom shapes, require minimal energy to produce, and fully compost at end-of-life. We've successfully used mycelium composites for protective packaging that performs better than expanded polystyrene while being completely biodegradable.

Advanced Recycled Composites

Recycled materials have evolved far beyond simple downcycling. New technologies can transform ocean plastic into premium textiles, waste agricultural fiber into structural composites, and reclaimed wood dust into beautiful solid surfaces.

For a furniture client, we developed a composite material that combines recycled ocean plastic with reclaimed wood fiber to create a material with the warmth of wood and the durability of plastic—all while removing waste from the environment.

Naturally Technical Materials

Some of the most exciting materials in sustainable design come from rethinking natural materials through a technical lens. Materials like densified bamboo, which offers strength comparable to steel; regenerated cellulose fibers, which can replace synthetic textiles; and engineered timber products that sequester carbon while outperforming conventional building materials.

In a recent luggage design, we replaced petroleum-based synthetic fabrics with Tencel™, a cellulose fiber made from sustainably harvested wood pulp using a closed-loop process. The resulting material proved more durable and moisture-wicking than the synthetics it replaced.

Material Selection: A Holistic Approach

Selecting sustainable materials requires looking beyond basic "green" credentials to consider the full lifecycle impact. Here's how we approach material selection:

Life Cycle Assessment

We evaluate materials not just on their composition but through their entire lifecycle: raw material extraction, processing, manufacturing, use phase, and end-of-life. This reveals that sometimes counter-intuitive choices are actually more sustainable. For instance, in some applications, an aluminum component with high recycled content and infinite recyclability might be more sustainable than a bioplastic with limited recycling infrastructure.

Durability and Repairability

Sometimes the most sustainable choice is simply a material that lasts longer or enables repair. For a consumer electronics client, we selected a slightly less "green" material for the outer casing because it allowed for modular repair, extending the product lifespan by years.

Regional Considerations

Material sustainability varies dramatically by region. A material with excellent recycling infrastructure in Europe might have none in North America. For global products, we map recycling capabilities across markets and either adapt materials by region or choose options with the broadest end-of-life solutions.

Balancing Sustainability with Performance and Aesthetics

The greatest sustainable materials innovation happens at the intersection of environmental responsibility, performance, and aesthetics. Successful sustainable products don't ask consumers to compromise.

Embracing Material Character

Rather than trying to make sustainable materials mimic conventional ones, we often find success in celebrating their unique aesthetic qualities. The slight color variations in recycled plastics can become a design feature rather than a flaw. The natural patterns in mycelium composites can create distinctive visual identity.

Testing at Scale

Many sustainable materials perform well in laboratory settings but face challenges in scaled manufacturing. We conduct extensive testing at production scale to identify and solve issues before launch. For a recent personal care product line, we worked with our manufacturing partner to adapt their equipment for processing a new bioplastic composite, resulting in both environmental benefits and reduced cycle times.

Cost Considerations

Sustainable materials often carry price premiums, particularly before reaching scale. We work with clients to deploy these materials strategically—perhaps using them in visible components where they provide brand value while using more conventional materials in internal components. As volumes increase, costs typically decrease, enabling broader implementation.

Case Study: Redesigning for Sustainability

A kitchen appliance client approached us to redesign their bestselling product with sustainability as a core objective. The original product used seven different types of plastic, making recycling nearly impossible, and had several metallic decorative elements that couldn't be separated from plastic components.

Our redesign:

  1. Reduced the types of plastic from seven to two, both widely recyclable
  2. Replaced decorative chrome elements with laser-etched patterns
  3. Used recycled aluminum for structural components, designed for easy separation
  4. Incorporated a bioplastic derived from waste cooking oil for non-structural parts
  5. Redesigned for disassembly with standard tools

The result was a product with 82% less virgin plastic, 35% recycled content overall, and 95% recyclability at end-of-life. Most importantly, these improvements came with a more contemporary aesthetic, improved durability, and only a 4% increase in manufacturing cost.

Looking Forward: Material Innovation

The sustainable materials landscape continues to evolve rapidly. Here are the developments I'm most excited about:

  • Carbon-negative materials that sequester more carbon than they emit during production
  • Programmed biodegradability in materials that remain stable during use but biodegrade quickly at end-of-life
  • Waste-to-value transformations that upcycle specific waste streams into premium materials
  • Closed-loop material systems where manufacturers maintain ownership of materials throughout product lifecycles

Conclusion

Sustainable materials are no longer just an environmental choice—they're driving some of the most exciting innovations in product design today. By approaching material selection holistically and embracing the unique properties of these new materials, designers can create products that are better for the planet and better for users.

The challenge for designers isn't simply substituting "green" materials into existing products; it's reimagining products around the distinctive properties and possibilities these materials offer. When we do that successfully, sustainability becomes not a constraint but a catalyst for innovation.
